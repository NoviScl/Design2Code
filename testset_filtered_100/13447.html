Part Two: Fishing Well When You Get There

By Dave Genz

Last time, we started going over a plan I use for fishing as well as possible when winter weather gets downright nasty. I suppose the definition might change depending on where you live, but everybody knows what you mean when you tell them it was ‘brutal’ out there.

Just to review, I mentioned last time that this is probably not the day to tackle a new lake. If you can, go to a lake you know well, and go right to your best spot.

-When we fish together, we often split up and check completely different areas, touching base with radios or cell phones, depending on distance. But on a really cold day, we’ll huddle together on the same spot, and leapfrog past one another as we look for active fish.

You decide ahead of time, for example, that you’re going to check out a deep weed edge. You find the edge, and then decide which direction you’re going to move.

After we leapfrog past each other and end up at the end, we can use one motorized transport to take us all back to the beginning. If we only have one ‘machine’ out there, one person hops in it and moves it to the front of the leapfrog line when we get some distance from the starting point.

I mentioned this before, but it bears repeating: wear a windproof, waterproof outer layer of clothing. If the wind can’t get at you, it’s amazing how warm you stay.

Put your heater up on top of a bucket. This requires extreme attention to detail.

Take great care to keep your auger blades free of ice. There’s no way you can drill a hole if you allow slush, snow and water to build up on the cutting surfaces.

I helped develop a new tool that StrikeMaster is going to make for next year that does a great job. It doesn’t even have a name yet, but it’s a tool that’s a peening hammer on one side and a chisel on the other.

Having said all this, the best way to solve this problem is to prevent it. When you finish drilling, make a clean spot on the ice, or put the auger up on your tailgate, and set it down without ever letting it contact snow, slush or water.

When you’re fishing, bank in your Fish Trap flaps. If the wind is flopping those flaps up and down, it’s much colder in there and you don’t have as much control over your presentation.

As you bank in, make good use of the auger slush. Put some on the flaps after you get inside.

Don’t take batteries for granted. Make sure they’re charged up, and keep them warm as long as you can before taking them out into the cold.
